Why it got rejected

The rejection is information

A rejected reinstatement appeal almost always means one of two things: the underlying violation was never actually fixed before you filed, or the documents you submitted were weak or inconsistent with the profile. Reviewers reject on inconsistency, not on how compelling the story is.

The most common failure is filing the appeal before correcting the field that caused the suspension — the reviewer opens the listing, sees the same violation, and denies it again. The second is documents whose business name or address don't match the profile character-for-character: a different suite number, “Co.” vs “Company,” an old address on a utility bill.

Before you do anything else: open the profile and the website side by side and find the inconsistency. If you can't name it, the next submission will fail for the same reason.

Do this next

Use the additional-review path — not a duplicate

  • 01
    Fix the actual violation first.Correct the field, remove the ineligible address, hide the address if you're a service-area business — whatever the real cause was. The form is not the fix; the profile is.
  • 02
    Submit the “additional review of a denied request” form.This is a separate, official path — one more shot with stronger and new evidence. Per Google's own documentation, any owner or manager may submit it, not just the person who filed the first appeal.
  • 03
    Bring more and better documents.License, utility bill, lease, signage photo — all matching the profile exactly. New evidence, not a re-send of what was already rejected.
  • 04
    If denied again and you're sure Google erred:Escalate through the Google Business Profile Help Community, where Gold/Platinum Product Experts can escalate cases internally. Be factual and specific about why the decision was wrong.

The hard limits

What you only get to do once

~60 min
After you submit the appeal and open the evidence form, the upload window is roughly one hour. Have files ready first.
~2
Widely reported (not officially stated by Google) effective limit: an initial appeal plus one additional review.
No SLA
Google publishes no guaranteed timeline — its doc only says it will email you a decision.
Resubmitting an identical appeal is auto-ignored — it does nothing but waste your one realistic remaining attempt. It is widely reported (not officially stated by Google) that, since late 2023, you effectively get an initial appeal plus one additional review; after that, further requests are typically ignored. Treat the additional-review submission as your last real shot and make it count.

Never do this

The duplicate-listing trap

Do not create a new listing to “start fresh.” A duplicate is a strong trust-negative signal, can permanently damage the entity, and does not escape the suspension — it compounds it. Repair the original profile through the official path; there is no clean restart.

How the appeals tool actually works

The reinstatement flow, step by step

The canonical tool is the Google Business Profile appeals tool (documented in Google support answer/4569145, “Fix suspended or disabled profiles,” and policy answer/13597551; colloquially the “reinstatement request form”). The flow is fixed, and most of the failures happen because owners rush a step:

  • 01
    Open the appeals tool and confirm the Google account.Use the account that actually owns or manages the suspended profile. The wrong account is a silent dead end.
  • 02
    Select the suspended profile and read the cited policy.The tool names a policy area. Read it — it tells you what the reviewer will look at, and what your evidence has to answer.
  • 03
    Submit Appeal, then click “Add evidence.”Submitting opens the evidence form. This is the moment the ~60-minute upload window starts.
  • 04
    Upload documents that match the profile exactly.Business name and address on every document must match the profile character-for-character. On a denied case this is the additional-review form — bring new and stronger documents, never a re-send.

The single biggest reason appeals fail is filing before the underlying violation is actually fixed — the reviewer sees the same problem and rejects again. The second is documents whose name or address don't match the profile exactly. Reviewers reject on inconsistency, not on story quality.

Why this is happening in 2026

The enforcement backdrop

In spring 2026 Google ran a series of AI-driven mass suspension sweeps — Sterling Sky logged a wave on May 5, 2026 tied to account restrictions, with industry reports of California high-risk service categories hit hardest in late April. The broader 2025–2026 trend, corroborated by Sterling Sky (Joy Hawkins' “Google Local Changes” log and the “Ultimate Playbook for GBP Suspensions”), Search Engine Roundtable (Barry Schwartz), and Near Media, is algorithmic, SpamBrain-style mass sweeps that hit even long-verified profiles.

Sterling Sky's change log through May 2026 also records review-reply moderation before publication, a Rating-Manipulation policy expansion, and deleted-review counts being surfaced — all signs of tighter, more automated enforcement. The practical takeaway: a verified, long-standing profile is no longer a shield. The defense is a clean, internally consistent profile and a correctly sequenced appeal — not seniority.
