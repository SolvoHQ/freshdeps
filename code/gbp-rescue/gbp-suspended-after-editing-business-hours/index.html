What actually triggered it

It probably wasn't the hours

You changed your opening hours, hit save, and within hours — sometimes minutes — the profile was suspended. The instinct is to blame the hours edit. In practice, editing business hours on its own rarely triggers a suspension.

What the automated spam filter actually reacts to is edit velocity and batched core-field edits. If, in the same session, you changed the business name, the address, and the primary category — or you made a rapid sequence of edits across the profile — the system kicks the listing back into automated re-review. The hours change is often just the last edit before the suspension lands, which is why it gets the blame.

Search Engine Roundtable (Barry Schwartz), covering the 2025–2026 suspension spike, has repeatedly warned owners to avoid making too many edits to a live profile, especially across the trust-sensitive fields (name, address, category). Spreading necessary edits out, and changing one field at a time, is materially safer than a single bulk update.

If you batched edits or edited repeatedly, that pattern — not the hours value — is the thing the appeal has to account for.

Do this now

The fix, in order

  • 01
    Stop editing. Completely.Every additional change while suspended deepens the re-review and gives the reviewer a moving target. Freeze the profile as it is.
  • 02
    Screenshot every field exactly as it stands.Name, address, phone, category, hours, website. You need a record of the truthful state before you touch anything in the appeal.
  • 03
    Appeal the original profile.Use the Google Business Profile appeals tool (the “reinstatement request form”): open it, confirm the Google account, select the suspended profile, read the cited policy, Submit Appeal, then click “Add evidence.” Do not create a new listing — a duplicate is trust-negative and can be permanent.
  • 04
    Document the legitimate reason for the change.A short, factual note: “Updated holiday hours” or “Corrected hours after seasonal change,” plus evidence the business is real and operating at the listed address.

How the appeals tool actually works

The reinstatement flow, step by step

The canonical tool is the Google Business Profile appeals tool (documented in Google support answer/4569145, “Fix suspended or disabled profiles,” and policy answer/13597551; colloquially the “reinstatement request form”). The flow is fixed, and most of the failures happen because owners rush a step:

  • 01
    Open the appeals tool and confirm the Google account.Use the account that actually owns or manages the suspended profile. The wrong account is a silent dead end.
  • 02
    Select the suspended profile and read the cited policy.The tool names a policy area. Read it — it tells you what the reviewer will look at, and what your evidence has to answer.
  • 03
    Submit Appeal, then click “Add evidence.”Submitting opens the evidence form. This is the moment the ~60-minute upload window starts.
  • 04
    Upload documents that match the profile exactly.Business name and address on every document must match the profile character-for-character. Where the change is relevant, a one-line factual note explaining the legitimate edit helps the reviewer.

The single biggest reason appeals fail is filing before the underlying violation is actually fixed — the reviewer sees the same problem and rejects again. The second is documents whose name or address don't match the profile exactly. Reviewers reject on inconsistency, not on story quality.

Why this is happening in 2026

The enforcement backdrop

In spring 2026 Google ran a series of AI-driven mass suspension sweeps — Sterling Sky logged a wave on May 5, 2026 tied to account restrictions, with industry reports of California high-risk service categories hit hardest in late April. The broader 2025–2026 trend, corroborated by Sterling Sky (Joy Hawkins' “Google Local Changes” log and the “Ultimate Playbook for GBP Suspensions”), Search Engine Roundtable (Barry Schwartz), and Near Media, is algorithmic, SpamBrain-style mass sweeps that hit even long-verified profiles.

Sterling Sky's change log through May 2026 also records review-reply moderation before publication, a Rating-Manipulation policy expansion, and deleted-review counts being surfaced — all signs of tighter, more automated enforcement. The practical takeaway: a verified, long-standing profile is no longer a shield. The defense is a clean, internally consistent profile and a correctly sequenced appeal — not seniority.
